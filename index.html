Unemployment rate and GDP of Malaysian States

In this visualization, we examine the relationship between unemployment rates and Gross Domestic Product (GDP) in a regional context. While it's natural to assume that rising unemployment rates might be accompanied by a significant decline in GDP, the data tells a different story. As we observe the choropleth map illustrating unemployment rates, we can discern regions with varying rates of unemployment. However, juxtaposed with the map depicting GDP, it becomes evident that these fluctuations in unemployment rates do not consistently correspond to changes in economic output. This intriguing finding challenges conventional economic expectations and highlights the complex and multifaceted nature of regional economic dynamics. In essence, the data reveals that, in some instances, higher unemployment rates are not synonymous with a proportional decrease in GDP, demonstrating the need for a nuanced understanding of the intricate factors that influence regional economies.

Employer's Contribution

This connected dot plot presents a compelling visual story of shifts in the occupational landscape from 2010 to 2016. The upward trajectory of male craft workers' numbers during this period is unmistakable, suggesting a noticeable growth in this occupational category. This rise could signify evolving job opportunities or changing workforce dynamics in the craft sector. Conversely, the connected dots for female employees paint a different picture. We observe that, over time, the dominance of women in certain occupational categories becomes evident. Specifically, female employees have a pronounced presence in general work, professional roles, service-oriented positions, and sales roles. This gendered distribution within these categories may be indicative of broader social, cultural, or economic factors shaping career choices and opportunities for women. As we follow the lines connecting these data points, we gain insight into the evolving dynamics of gender and employment across various occupational sectors. This visualization invites us to contemplate the underlying factors that have driven these shifts in male and female employment patterns and highlights the potential for further research into the reasons behind these trends.
